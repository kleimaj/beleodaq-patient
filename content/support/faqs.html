BELEODAQ FAQs

Find answers to some frequently asked questions about BELEODAQ.

BELEODAQ (belinostat) is a prescription anticancer medicine that can help treat people with peripheral T-cell lymphoma (PTCL) that returns or does not respond to other therapies.1 Your doctor may recommend BELEODAQ after you have tried other anticancer medicines.1

This indication is approved under accelerated approval based on tumor response rate and duration of response. An improvement in survival or disease-related symptoms has not been established. Continued approval for this indication may be contingent upon veriﬁcation and description of clinical beneﬁt in the conﬁrmatory trial.1

It is not known if BELEODAQ is safe and effective in children.1

Learn more about BELEODAQ

If you have PTCL that comes back or does not respond to other therapies, BELEODAQ may be an option for you. There are a number of treatments for relapsed or refractory PTCL.4 Asking questions about what to expect from treatment and talking with your doctor about what’s important to you can help you learn if BELEODAQ may be right for you.

See a list of questions to ask your doctor

PTCL starts when a change in the DNA of a normal T cell causes it to divide too quickly and grow out of control.3 BELEODAQ blocks the action of enzymes called HDACs that can affect how cells divide and grow. BELEODAQ causes a chemical change that can stop cancer cells from growing and cause them to die.1,3 BELEODAQ is the only HDAC inhibitor FDA approved for relapsed or refractory PTCL.1,9

Learn more about BELEODAQ and PTCL

BELEODAQ was shown to be effective against the most common subtypes of PTCL.1,4

Learn more about clinical trial results with BELEODAQ

BELEODAQ is given as a short, 30-minute IV infusion on days 1 through 5 followed by 16 days off treatment. One cycle of BELEODAQ is equal to 3 weeks. A new cycle on days 1 through 5 can be repeated every 3 weeks.1

You will receive treatment at an outpatient infusion center.

Learn more about taking BELEODAQ

During your treatment, your doctor will do blood tests to see how BELEODAQ is affecting you. If you are responding to and tolerating BELEODAQ, your physician may start a new cycle every 3 weeks.1

Learn more about monitoring your treatment

In clinical trials, most patients had side effects that were generally mild to moderate in severity. The most common side effects were nausea, fatigue, fever, anemia, and vomiting.1,2

Serious side effects may occur with BELEODAQ.
