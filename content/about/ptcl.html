What is PTCL?

Peripheral T-cell lymphoma (PTCL) is a rare type of non-Hodgkin lymphoma (NHL). PTCL is a cancer of the immune system that starts when a type of white blood cell called a T lymphocyte, or T cell, begins growing out of control.3,4

It is called “peripheral” because the cancer starts in T cells outside the bone marrow. These cells may be found in the spleen, gastrointestinal (GI) tract, skin, or lymph nodes.3

T cells are an important part of the body’s immune response to ﬁghting illness or infection.3

How PTCL starts

PTCL starts when a change in the DNA of a normal T cell causes it to divide too quickly and grow out of control. This allows the fast-growing cancer cells to build up in the body and live longer than normal cells.3

DNA instructions coded inside the chromosomes of each T cell control how it behaves.3,5 DNA wraps around proteins called histones that give chromosomes their shape.6 An enzyme called HDAC (histone deacetylase) can change the way DNA wraps around the histones. This can affect how cells divide and grow.3

PTCL has many subtypes

You may know that PTCL has many subtypes.4 Naming the subtypes helps doctors describe the different types of cancerous T cells and their location in the body. Your doctor may talk to you about your PTCL subtype.

Most PTCLs are aggressive

Most PTCLs are aggressive (fast-moving), and patients with PTCL are likely to experience relapse (meaning that the disease returns) after they had first responded to a treatment. In some cases, patients with PTCL do not respond to treatment, and this is known as refractory PTCL.3,6,7 Although there is no standard approach to treating relapsed or refractory PTCL, a number of treatments are available.8
